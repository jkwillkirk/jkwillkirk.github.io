The Digital Archaeology of Mansfield District Council: An Infrastructural Assessment

Executive Summary

This report presents a comprehensive archaeological assessment of the digital infrastructure, processes, and culture of Mansfield District Council. Moving beyond a surface-level audit, this analysis excavates the layers of technology that have been deposited over decades, revealing a complex digital landscape characterised by a modern citizen-facing platform built upon a bedrock of older, resilient, but often disconnected back-office systems.

The council's digital estate can be understood as a geological stratigraphy. The uppermost layer, the "Jadu Stratum," dates from a significant modernisation project in 2019 and provides a clean, user-centric interface for many services.[1] However, this modern veneer rests on deeper, older strata of core business systems, particularly in critical areas such as Council Tax and Housing Benefits. These legacy systems, while robust, were designed in a different technological era and impose significant constraints on the organisation's agility and ability to deliver truly seamless, end-to-end digital services. Evidence points to these core systems being provided by major local government vendor NEC Software Solutions, creating a powerful structural dependency.[2]

The primary thesis of this report is that the council's most significant challenges do not stem from a lack of modern technology, but from the systemic friction that occurs at the interfaces between these different technological layers. This friction is evident in fractured user journeys that begin online but require citizens to revert to telephone calls or paper-based correspondence to complete a task, such as the inability to cancel a bulky waste collection online.[3] It is also manifest in the cultural divides between different departmental "tribes"â€”from the digitally fluent "Envoys" in Communications, who manage the council's active social media presence [4], to the risk-averse "Custodians" in Finance, who rely on static PDF documents for official reporting.[5] These varying relationships with technology create inconsistencies in service delivery. Furthermore, significant "tectonic faults" exist where the council's digital realm meets the physical world, most notably in its strategy of discouraging in-person visits to the Civic Centre [6] and its apparent outsourcing of responsibility for digital inclusion to third-party organisations.[7]

Finally, an analysis of the council's strategic documents reveals a distinct narrative arc. The 2019 platform modernisation, driven by the need to replace a "legacy CMS" with "considerable customisation" [1], represented a moment when Information Technology was wielded as a strategic weapon to drive transformation. The current strategic posture, however, has shifted. The "Towards 2030" strategy frames technology primarily as an external-facing piece of economic infrastructure to be maintained, rather than a continuous engine for organisational change.[8] This shift from a transformative to a maintenance-oriented mindset, reflected in the support-focused roles of the internal ICT team [9], presents the greatest long-term risk to the council's ability to adapt and evolve in an increasingly digital world. This report concludes with a series of targeted recommendations designed to address these structural issues, realign the council's operational reality with its digital ambitions, and identify the next most valuable areas for technological investment.

Section 1: Mapping the Strata: The Technological Sediment of Citizen Services

An archaeological excavation of Mansfield District Council's citizen-facing services reveals a technological landscape composed of distinct layers, or strata, deposited over time. Each layer corresponds to a different era of technological thinking and investment, resulting in a complex and often inconsistent user experience. This section maps these strata, from the deepest legacy bedrock to the most modern digital veneers, to understand the composition of the council's core service offerings.

1.1 The Bedrock Services: Council Tax and Housing Benefits

The services of Council Tax and Housing Benefits represent the deepest, most resilient, and most structurally significant technological layer within the council. These are high-volume, high-risk, and legally mandated functions that form the financial bedrock of the organisation. The evidence suggests these services are delivered through a classic "Modern-on-Legacy" hybrid model.

The visible surface is the modern Jadu Content Management System (CMS), implemented circa 2019, which provides a user-friendly "shop window" for citizens.[1] Through this portal, residents can access a suite of online forms for Council Tax, including making payments, applying for discounts, and notifying the council of a change of address.[10] This modern front-end, however, is a veneer. The data captured through these forms is passed to a much older, more robust back-end system that serves as the definitive system of record. Multiple artefacts point to this core system being provided by NEC Software Solutions UK Ltd, a major vendor in the local government sector. The council explicitly notes its partnership with NEC to conduct its Single Persons Discount review, even providing a dedicated helpline number for the NEC-led review process.[2] Further excavation reveals that the online claim form for Housing Benefit is also an NEC system, as detailed in the council's "Electronic Claiming Directive".[11] Given that Revenues and Benefits systems are typically sold as integrated suites, it is almost certain that a single, core NEC system underpins both of these critical services.

This deep integration with a single vendor creates a powerful structural dependency. The council is not merely using software; it is operating within a vendor-locked ecosystem that represents the most immovable layer of its technological geology. Replacing such a system would be a high-risk, high-cost "heart transplant" operation. The 2019 Jadu project was, therefore, a pragmatic and logical decision to improve the citizen interface without attempting to disturb this deeply embedded legacy core.

The pre-digital origins of this core system are revealed in the fractured user journey for Housing Benefit claims. The instructions on the council's website explicitly state: "If you don't have all the proof don't delay submitting your form to us. You can send proof later".[11] This separation of the initial claim from the submission of evidence is a direct digitisation of a paper-based workflow. In an analogue era, a citizen would submit a form to "get in the queue," and an officer would subsequently request the necessary paperwork. A truly modern, end-to-end digital system would integrate evidence uploads into a single, seamless transaction. The current process creates ambiguity for the citizen ("Is my claim progressing? Have you received my documents?") and increases the administrative burden on council staff who must manually match separately submitted documents to the correct case file. This is a clear artefact of a legacy system dictating the shape of a modern digital process.

1.2 The Logistical Layer: Waste and Environmental Services

The stratum associated with logistical services, such as waste collection, is a "Hybrid Digital/Analogue" layer. Here, modern digital processes for simple requests coexist with a reliance on manual, analogue processes for exceptions and modifications. This friction is a key source of inefficiency and user frustration.

The Jadu front-end effectively captures new service requests. Citizens can use a clear, well-structured online form to book and pay for a bulky item collection, with a transparent pricing structure provided upfront (Â£15.00 for one item, Â£27.00 for two or three).[3] This represents a successful one-way flow of data from the citizen to the council. However, the digital process breaks down as soon as a citizen needs to modify an existing request. To cancel or rearrange a collection, the user is explicitly instructed to telephone the council on 01623 463463; there is no online self-service option for this action.[3]

This mandatory reversion to the telephone channel is a significant artefact. It implies that the back-end scheduling and logistics systemâ€”whether an in-house application or a system operated by the council's waste contractor, Veolia [12]â€”is not integrated for two-way communication with the public-facing website. The telephone operator who handles the cancellation request is, in effect, acting as a "human API." They must manually receive the instruction via voice and then enter it into a separate, disconnected logistics system.

This creates a direct contradiction with the council's stated strategic goal. The "Contact us" page encourages digital contact because it is "much quicker and cheaper than other contact methods".[6] Yet, for this common scenario, the system forces the user onto the most expensive and time-consuming channel. This is not a policy choice but a clear indicator of a technological limitation. The cost and complexity of fully integrating the web front-end with the back-end logistics system were likely deemed prohibitive during the initial Jadu implementation. As a result, the council continues to absorb the ongoing operational cost of the "human API" in its contact centreâ€”a form of hidden technical debt that manifests as a recurring operational expense.

1.3 The Regulatory Engine: Planning and Licensing

The Planning service constitutes its own "Specialised Digital Ecosystem." This is a mature technological stratum, likely older than the 2019 Jadu platform, but one that has been maintained and developed over many years to handle the unique complexities of regulatory workflows.

The council provides a dedicated public-facing portal for planning functions, allowing users to search for applications, submit comments, and apply for permission.[13] This portal is integrated with national services like the Planning Portal, indicating a level of sophistication beyond a simple web form.[14] However, the underlying processes remain deeply document-centric. The website is replete with Supplementary Planning Documents (SPDs), monitoring reports, and policy documents, all published as static PDFs.[15] This shows that the core logic of the system is based on the management of official documents rather than the manipulation of live data.

A key feature of this ecosystem is its "multi-modal ingestion" capability for public comments on planning applications. Citizens can submit their views via the online portal, by email, or by traditional post.[13] While this approach maximises accessibility, it creates a significant administrative overhead and prevents the full efficiency gains of a digital-by-default process. A comment submitted via the online form can be automatically validated and entered into the planning database. In contrast, a comment arriving by email or post must be read, interpreted, and manually keyed into the system by a council officer. This policy choice to preserve pre-digital workflows means the entire back-office process must be designed to accommodate the least efficient input method, requiring a permanent human-staffed triage function. The partnership with Erewash Council for the delivery of the building control service further highlights the specialised, and sometimes fragmented, nature of this regulatory technology landscape.[16]

1.4 The Fallow Ground: Underdeveloped Digital Services (Pest Control)

In contrast to the developed digital offerings in other areas, some services occupy what can only be described as "fallow ground." Pest control is the prime example of a service that remains technologically "Pre-Digital" or has been fully outsourced without deep integration.

While other local authorities offer dedicated online booking and payment systems for pest control visits, with clear pricing structures [17], Mansfield District Council does not. Instead, citizens are directed to a generic "Report environmental issue" form, which includes "vermin" as one of a list of possible problems.[18] There is no specific, transactional service page for booking a visit, viewing prices, or making a payment. This forces residents to either use the non-specific form, which likely triggers a manual triage process, or turn directly to the private sector, which is actively serving the area with offers of 24/7 emergency call-outs and free surveys.[19]

The absence of a dedicated digital service for pest control is a telling artefact. It indicates that the service is either considered non-core, has too low a volume to justify investment in a digital pathway, or is fully outsourced to a contractor who does not offer an integrated booking system. The council possesses the core technological capability to build such a serviceâ€”the Jadu platform supports e-forms and payments, as demonstrated by the bulky waste collection service. The decision not to apply this capability here represents a conscious or unconscious gap in the council's digital service portfolio. This strategic void reveals an internal "invest vs. ignore" calculation that has left this particular service area undeveloped in the digital landscape.

Section 2: Identifying the Tribes: An Ethnography of Departmental Digital Cultures

The technological strata of the council are inhabited by distinct departmental "tribes," each with its own unique culture and relationship with technology. These internal subcultures, shaped by professional norms, regulatory pressures, and historical workflows, are a critical factor in determining how technology is adopted and utilised. Understanding these tribesâ€”whether they are "digital natives" driving change or "digital settlers" adapting to new toolsâ€”is essential to diagnosing the human causes of technical faults.

2.1 The Custodians (Finance)

The Finance department embodies the culture of the "Custodian." Their relationship with technology is defined by the overriding principles of accuracy, control, compliance, and risk aversion. The public-facing artefacts produced by this tribe are formal, statutory, and document-based. The council's Annual Statement of Accounts, for instance, is published as a large, monolithic PDF file, with the draft 2024/25 accounts being an 8.37MB download.[5] This format is perfectly suited to their cultural imperatives: it is static, secure, and serves as an immutable official record that mimics a printed document, ideal for audit trails.[22]

This tribe can be classified as "Digital Settlers" of a highly conservative nature. They use technology not as a tool for innovation or exploration, but as a means to execute established, legally mandated processes with precision. Their preference for the static PDF report over more dynamic forms of data presentation is a powerful cultural indicator. A "digital native" finance culture might explore open data standards, interactive public-facing dashboards, or APIs to allow for real-time financial monitoring. The absence of these tools reveals a culture that prioritises the final, audited report over the live, dynamic data . This document-centric worldview means that vast quantities of valuable financial data are effectively locked away in formats that are difficult for other parts of the organisation to analyse or reuse, creating a significant barrier to the council adopting more agile, data-driven decision-making.

2.2 The Envoys (Communications)

The Communications team represents the council's "Envoy" tribe and is the closest in culture to being "Digital Natives." Their professional world exists almost entirely within the digital realm. They are the managers of the council's primary digital shop windowâ€”the websiteâ€”and its active presence on social media platforms including Facebook, X (formerly Twitter), Instagram, and LinkedIn.[4] They are fluent in the language of user engagement, content management, and digital analytics. As the authors of the "Communications and Engagement Strategy 2023," their role is to "champion and promote the district and communicate a positive image" in the digital sphere.[23]

This team was undoubtedly a key stakeholder in the 2019 Jadu project, which was focused on modernising the public-facing digital experience.[1] However, their digital fluency can mask the less-developed capabilities of other departments. They actively promote a vision of a seamless, digital-first council, encouraging residents to use online channels because they are "quicker and cheaper".[6] This creates the potential for a significant promise-delivery gap. When the slick, modern "front door" managed by the Communications team leads to a clunky, analogue-interrupted "back room" process (such as the telephone-only cancellation for bulky waste), the result is citizen frustration. The "native" culture of the Comms team may be out of sync with the "settler" cultures of the operational departments, creating a source of cultural, not just technical, friction.

2.3 The Gatekeepers (Planning & Housing)

The Planning and Housing departments can be understood as "Gatekeeper" tribes. Their culture is that of "Specialised Settlers," shaped by the need to manage complex, high-stakes regulatory frameworks. They are not technological innovators, but they are competent and often deeply knowledgeable users of the specialised, domain-specific systems required to do their jobs.

Their adoption of technology is pragmatic and process-driven, often catalysed by external pressure. This is starkly illustrated by the Housing department. A recent job advertisement for a "Housing IT Development Support Officer" is explicitly framed in the context of responding to a "C2 judgement from the Regulator of Social Housing".[21] The purpose of this new role is to support an "improvement and transformation plan" with a focus on developing IT systems and, critically, "ensuring the integrity of the data to support business delivery, with a focus on data quality and accuracy".[21]

This is a revelatory artefact. It shows that a significant external regulatory failure is the direct driver for technological investment and a renewed focus on digital maturity within the Housing service. The department is not investing proactively to innovate; it is investing reactively to remediate a known compliance and data quality problem identified by an outside body. This suggests a pattern where digital improvement is driven by crisis rather than strategy. It also raises the question of what similar, unaddressed data quality issues may be lurking in other departments that are not subject to the same level of intense external scrutiny.

2.4 The Engineers (ICT Services)

The central ICT team represents the "Engineer" tribe, and their culture appears to be one of "Digital Maintainers." An analysis of their recruitment provides a clear window into their primary function and mindset. Job descriptions for roles such as "ICT Support Technician" are heavily focused on reactive, operational tasks: providing first-line support to end users, investigating and resolving hardware and software issues, and configuring and deploying desktop equipment.[9]

The required skills are practical and support-oriented: "Experience in the investigation and resolution of end user ICT issues and queries" and the "ability to work under pressure".[24] While a "keen interest in ICT and new technologies" is listed as desirable, the core duties are centred on maintaining the stability of the existing digital estateâ€”in short, "keeping the lights on." There is no evidence in their recruitment of a focus on proactive, strategic roles such as digital business analysis, user experience design, or service transformation leadership.

This creates a potential strategy/execution gap. The council's high-level "Towards 2030" strategy contains aspirational language about the need to "embrace technology and technological advances".[8] However, the internal ICT team is structured and staffed primarily for maintenance, not for transformation. This suggests that major change initiatives, like the 2019 Jadu project, are likely treated as discrete, one-off projects, perhaps heavily reliant on external partners like the "digital transformation service partner Methods" mentioned in the Jadu announcement.[1] Once such a project is complete, the internal team is left to manage and maintain the new system. This two-speed structureâ€”a slow, steady "business-as-usual" internal team and a periodic, project-based "transformation" functionâ€”makes a culture of continuous, iterative digital improvement very difficult to foster and sustain.

Section 3: Locating the Tectonic Faults: Stress Points Between the Digital and Physical Realms

The stability of any technological landscape depends on the integrity of the connections between its different parts. Within Mansfield District Council, significant "tectonic faults" exist at the points where the digital realm meets the physical world of citizens, buildings, and analogue processes. These stress points are where digital systems often fail, requiring human intervention and generating a disproportionate number of support issues.

3.1 The Civic Centre as the Primary Fault Line

The council's main office, the Civic Centre, represents the primary fault line between its digital-first ambitions and the physical reality of citizen needs. The corporate "Contact us" page is explicit in its channel shift strategy, actively encouraging residents to use digital services to "save you an unnecessary journey" and because they are "much quicker and cheaper".[6] Face-to-face meetings with council officers are strongly discouraged without a prior appointment. The stated process for a citizen arriving without an appointment is for the Customer Services team to attempt to resolve the enquiry using online or telephone channelsâ€”effectively, to redirect the citizen back to the very channels they may have already tried and failed to use.[6]

This strategy fundamentally repositions the Civic Centre from a primary service delivery channel to a point of exception handling. The default process is intended to be digital, with the physical building serving as the fallback. The success of this model is entirely dependent on the quality and completeness of the council's digital services. The friction points identified in Section 1â€”such as the inability to cancel a bulky waste collection onlineâ€”will inevitably manifest as support demand at the Civic Centre's front desk.

This creates the risk of a "digital rebound" effect, where a poor or incomplete online experience drives citizens to make more determined, and often more frustrated, in-person visits. The staff at the Civic Centre are placed on the front line of every failure in the digital estate. Their primary role in this new model is to act as the human safety net for digital service failures, mediating between a resident's needs and the limitations of the council's online offerings. This places them under immense pressure and can lead to a negative experience for both staff and citizens.

3.2 The Persistence of Analogue Channels

Despite the digital-first rhetoric, analogue communication channels remain deeply embedded and, in some cases, mandatory components of key service processes. The telephone is not merely an alternative contact method; it is a required tool to complete specific tasks. It is the only way to cancel or rearrange a bulky waste collection [3], the specified method for booking an appointment with a Financial Inclusion officer [7], and the primary contact channel for the helpline managing the Council Tax Single Persons Discount review.[2]

Similarly, paper-based correspondence has not been eliminated. It remains a valid and accepted channel for submitting comments on planning applications [13], and the process for submitting evidence for Housing Benefit claims implicitly accommodates the sending of physical documents.[11] Each of these instances represents a crack in the digital facadeâ€”a tectonic fault where a modern digital process grinds to a halt and reverts to an older, manual one.

These analogue fallbacks should not be viewed as a failure of citizens to adopt digital ways of working, but rather as a failure of the council's systems to be fully digital from end to end. They represent processes that were not, or could not be, fully re-engineered during the 2019 modernisation project. They are reliable indicators of the true age and limitations of the back-office systems that underpin these services. Each mandatory phone call or acceptance of a paper form is a source of hidden operational cost and a point of friction that undermines the council's efficiency goals.

3.3 The Digital Access Divide

For a council pursuing an aggressive channel shift strategy, there is a remarkable and concerning lack of information regarding support for digital access within its own public buildings. The research material contains no mention of public-access computers, free public Wi-Fi, or digital assistance services being available at the Mansfield Civic Centre.[6] Searches for such facilities at Mansfield District Council are fruitless, instead returning results for unrelated municipalities in the United States.[25]

This omission represents a major tectonic fault between the council's digital ambitions and the reality of digital exclusion that exists within any community. The strategy appears to assume that all citizens have the necessary equipment, connectivity, skills, and confidence to interact with the council online. For those who do not, the council's primary response seems to be referral to external partners. The website directs residents needing advice and support to organisations like the Citizens Advice Bureau, which has a presence at the Civic Centre but is a separate entity.[7]

By doing so, the council is effectively outsourcing the responsibility for bridging the digital divide. While partnerships with the voluntary sector are valuable, the absence of any visible in-house support at the primary physical point of contactâ€”the Civic Centreâ€”suggests that digital inclusion is not treated as a core component of the council's own service delivery. This creates a fragile ecosystem. If partner organisations lose funding or capacity, the council's digital-first strategy will fail its most vulnerable residents. This will either exclude them from accessing essential services or drive them back towards more expensive and inefficient channels, thereby undermining the entire premise of the channel shift strategy.

Section 4: Decoding the Blueprints: The Council's Strategic View of Technology

The final stage of this archaeological analysis is to interpret the council's strategic documentsâ€”its blueprintsâ€”to understand its fundamental philosophy towards technology. These documents reveal how the organisation views Information Technology: as a transformative strategic weapon, or as a simple utility cost to be managed. The evidence points to a significant evolution in this view over the past five years.

4.1 The Rosetta Stone: The 2019 Jadu Platform Modernisation

The press release from 30th May 2019, announcing the council's selection of the Jadu Continuum platform, is a pivotal documentâ€”a "Rosetta Stone" that allows us to decode the council's intentions at a key moment of change.[1] The language in this announcement is unambiguously transformative. It outlines a clear strategic project with citizen-centric and efficiency-driven goals.

The stated problem was a "legacy CMS" that had become overly complex due to "considerable customisation," creating "additional maintenance challenges".[1] The solution was to invest in a modern, cloud-based platform to deliver a "fresh new look" and "smooth experience" for residents. Key objectives included implementing "single sign-on capability" to reduce user friction and, crucially, enabling better self-service to "reduce avoidable contact in the form of chasing phone calls and contact centre drop-ins".[1]

This document captures a moment when IT was clearly viewed as a strategic weapon. The council was using a major technology investment to solve core business problems: improving the customer experience and driving down operational costs. The project represents a "big bang" approach to transformationâ€”a great leap forward designed to replace a major piece of infrastructure in a single, defined initiative. While effective in delivering a visible upgrade, this project-based model of change carries the risk that transformation is seen as a one-off event rather than a continuous process.

4.2 The Official Doctrine: The 'Towards 2030' Strategy

The council's current high-level blueprint, the refreshed "Towards 2030: A Strategy for Mansfield," reveals a significant shift in tone and focus.[26] While the foreword acknowledges that the response to the pandemic created "new opportunities to work in different and more agile ways," [27] this lesson does not translate into a specific, technology-led programme of internal transformation.

Instead, technology is mentioned primarily in a supporting role for wider economic goals. Within the five main themes of the strategy, technology appears under the ambition for "Our economy." Here, a key priority is to "Support the development of infrastructure, embracing technology and technological advances".[8] This framing is revealing. Technology is positioned as a piece of external-facing infrastructure, akin to roads or commercial premises, that supports the district's economic growth. This contrasts sharply with the 2019 project's internal focus on transforming the council's own service delivery. The strategic attention appears to have pivoted outwards. Having "fixed" the website, the leadership's focus seems to have moved on to broader economic development, and the internal digital agenda is no longer the primary driver.

This is further reinforced by the Nottinghamshire-wide "Digital Connectivity Framework," which speaks of high-speed internet as a "necessity" for modern life and economic activity.[28] The council's strategy reflects this broader view of digital as an economic enabler for the region, but it lacks a specific, corresponding vision for the ongoing transformation of its own internal operations and services.

4.3 Synthesis: From Strategic Weapon to Utility Cost

Synthesising these blueprints reveals a clear narrative arc in the council's view of technology. In the period leading up to 2019, IT was treated as a strategic weapon to address the clear and pressing problems of a complex legacy website and inefficient contact methods. The Jadu project was the decisive battle in that campaign.

However, the current strategic posture suggests that, having won that battle, the council has shifted its view. The combination of a high-level strategy that frames technology as external infrastructure [8] and an internal ICT team that is structured and recruited for maintenance and support [9] points to a new philosophy. IT is no longer seen as the primary agent of radical change; it is now viewed as a utility to be managed, a set of services to be maintained, and a cost to be controlled.

The transformation of 2019 is at risk of being seen as a completed event rather than the beginning of a continuous process of improvement. The "lights are on" in the new digital Civic Centre, but there is no evidence of a blueprint on the table for the next phase of architectural redesign or internal process re-engineering. This shift from a transformative to an operational mindset is the most significant strategic finding of this archaeological assessment.

Conclusion and Strategic Recommendations

The digital archaeology of Mansfield District Council reveals a complex, multi-layered organisation. It has successfully implemented a modern digital veneer, providing citizens with a clean and accessible front door to many services. However, this veneer rests upon a bedrock of legacy systems and analogue-era processes that create significant friction, inefficiency, and cultural divides. The council's most pressing challenge is not the absence of technology, but the management of the stress points at the interfaces between its different technological strata, its departmental tribes, and its digital and physical realms. Having made a significant transformative leap in 2019, the organisation's strategic posture has now become more conservative, viewing IT as a utility to be maintained rather than a continuous driver of change.

To address these structural findings and build upon the foundations of the 2019 modernisation, the following strategic recommendations are proposed:

  1. Refocus the Digital Strategy on Continuous Improvement: The "Towards 2030" strategy should be augmented with a dedicated internal Digital Transformation roadmap. This roadmap must re-establish the principle that the 2019 platform project was the beginning, not the end, of the transformation journey. It should shift the organisational mindset from a "big bang" project model to one of agile, continuous, and iterative improvement of end-to-end service journeys.
  2. Prioritise Investment in Eliminating "Human APIs": The council should conduct a systematic audit of all processes that require citizens to revert to telephone or paper channels. Each instance represents a "human API" and a source of inefficiency. A cost-benefit analysis should be undertaken, starting with the bulky waste collection service, to prioritise investment in the back-end integrations necessary to create truly seamless, self-service digital journeys. Eliminating these friction points will deliver the cost savings originally envisioned by the channel shift strategy.
  3. Establish a Cross-Departmental Digital Practice Group: To bridge the cultural divides between the "tribes," the council should create a formal, cross-departmental digital practice group. This group should be championed by the "digital native" Communications team but must include representatives from the "Custodian" (Finance), "Gatekeeper" (Housing, Planning), and "Engineer" (ICT) tribes. Its mandate would be to co-design service improvements, share digital skills and best practices, and act as a centre of excellence for user-centric design within the organisation.
  4. Address the Digital Inclusion Chasm Immediately: The council's "digital first" strategy is critically undermined by the lack of in-house support for digitally excluded residents. It is strongly recommended that a digital inclusion service be established as a matter of urgency at the Civic Centre. This must include, at a minimum, free public Wi-Fi, a small number of public-access computer terminals, and designated staff trained to provide basic digital assistance. Treating digital inclusion as a core part of service delivery, rather than outsourcing it by default, is a critical dependency for the long-term success and fairness of the council's entire digital strategy.

Intelligence Briefing: Reading the Political Barometer at Mansfield District Council

Introduction: The Technician as Strategist

This briefing deconstructs the operational environment of Mansfield District Council (MDC) to provide the strategic intelligence necessary for a senior technology role. The central thesis is that the council operates within a fundamental and defining contradiction: a sustained period of severe operational budget constraint set against a politically driven, ambitious, and capital-intensive regeneration agenda. The Information and Communications Technology (ICT) department is positioned at the precise nexus of this conflict. It serves simultaneously as a shock absorber for austerity and as a critical enabler of the council's future vision. Within this context, an ageing server is not merely an instance of technical debt; it is a fossilised record of a past budget cut, a tangible artefact of political and financial history.

The analysis that follows is structured to move beyond a superficial reading of the council's technical needs. It interprets the ICT environment as a political barometer, sensitive to the pressures of municipal finance, public sentiment, and the strategic priorities of the council's leadership. The report is divided into four key sections. The first examines the financial landscape, dissecting the dual realities of a squeezed operational budget and a flush capital programme for regeneration. The second provides a framework for translating technical solutions into the language of civic value, aligning ICT proposals with the council's explicit strategic goals and residents' stated priorities. The third assesses the role of ICT in the democratic process itself, identifying a critical gap in transparency that represents a significant political opportunity. The final section calibrates the conversation to the key players within the council's hierarchy, analysing their roles and responsibilities to define the precise nature of the position being filled.

By deconstructing these pressures, this briefing provides the necessary context to position a candidate not as a simple technician tasked with maintenance, but as a strategic partner who understands the political landscape, speaks the language of civic benefit, and can deliver technology solutions that align with the core ambitions of Mansfield District Council.

Section 1: The Financial Landscape: Navigating Austerity and Ambition

To understand the ICT department at Mansfield District Council is to first understand the powerful and often conflicting financial currents that shape every decision. The council's financial posture is not monolithic; it is a bifurcated reality where deep-seated austerity in day-to-day operations coexists with a bold, multi-million-pound investment in the district's future. This dynamic creates a complex environment where resources are simultaneously scarce and, for the right projects, abundant.

1.1 Macro-Pressures: The Squeeze on the General Fund

The council's operational budget is under sustained and significant pressure, a situation shaped by both national policy and local economic conditions. The Cabinet's proposal for the 2025/2026 budget explicitly frames the current challenges against a backdrop of "15 years of continued government reductions to council finances".[29] This long-term trend of reduced central funding has created a structural deficit that the council must actively manage each year.

This historical pressure has been compounded by recent economic shocks. The council's 2024 Budget Consultation report states that it is facing its "most difficult budget pressures for many years," citing large increases in inflation that drive up costs for energy, fuel, and materials.[30] This is not an abstract financial problem; it has direct consequences for service delivery. The cost-of-living crisis has increased public demand for council services, particularly financial and housing support for tenants and residents. At the same time, the council must budget for mandatory pay awards and make provisions for potential bad debts from unpaid commercial rent, further straining its resources.[30] A further pressure point is the increase in National Insurance contributions that employers must pay from April 2025.[29]

The result is a tangible and persistent funding gap. The council anticipates a shortfall of over Â£3 million by March 2026.[29] For the immediate 2025/26 financial year, the budget report shows a projected deficit on the General Fund of Â£237,452, with further annual deficits expected in the subsequent two years.[31] In January 2025, it was reported that the council had successfully plugged a Â£2.2 million gap for the 2024/25 financial year, but this required cuts to services and the use of over Â£220,000 from its reserves.[32]

1.2 The Council's Response: A Strategy for Financial Resilience

In response to these pressures, the council's leadership has adopted a multi-pronged strategy focused on fiscal responsibility and income generation. The Executive Mayor of Mansfield, Andy Abrahams, has been clear about the necessity of this approach, stating, "We must prioritise and, possibly, reduce some services... it is necessary to ensure the council's long-term stability".[32] This sentiment is echoed by the Portfolio Holder for Corporate and Finance, Cllr Craig Whitby, who has promised to make the council "as efficient as possible" while exploring "all avenues for raising new income from sources other than council tax".[32]

To achieve a balanced budget for 2025/26, the Cabinet has proposed several specific measures. The most significant is an annual increase in council tax by 2.99%.[29] This is a politically sensitive move, but one that appears to have a degree of public acceptance; the 2024 budget consultation found that 57% of participants were willing to accept such an increase.[30] Other proposals include reducing member ward allowances, gradually removing rents in kind, and finding Â£49,000 in savings from the "health, communities and insight establishment".[29] This latter point is particularly telling, as it indicates a focus on finding efficiencies in non-frontline, back-office functionsâ€”a category into which core ICT operations often fall.

1.3 The Dominant Political Narrative: Multi-Million Pound Regeneration

Contrasting sharply with the narrative of operational austerity is the council's primary public-facing message: one of ambitious, large-scale regeneration. This is where the political energy and, crucially, significant financial resources are being directed. While the General Fund is squeezed, the council is actively deploying tens of millions of pounds from its capital programme, heavily supplemented by UK Government grants, to transform the district.

This regeneration agenda is not a peripheral activity; it is the council's central story, a key part of its "Town Centre Masterplan".[33] The portfolio of projects is extensive and high-profile, designed to reshape the town centre and key residential areas:

  • Mansfield Connect: A flagship project to transform the former Beales department store into a multi-agency civic hub that will also serve as the new council headquarters. Supported by a Â£25 million UK Government grant, the project is expected to see demolition of connecting bridges commence in January 2025, with the new hub fully operational by February 2027.[33]
  • White Hart Street: An investment of over Â£17 million of council cash to redevelop a derelict site with new social housing (a mix of family houses and apartments for older people) and commercial spaces, connecting key parts of the town centre.[33]
  • Bellamy Estate: A Â£7.7 million housing-led regeneration of one of the district's most deprived areas. The project, which broke ground in August 2024, will deliver 22 new energy-efficient council homes, a new parade of shops, a community orchard, and a new through-road, with overall work expected to conclude in October 2025.[33]
  • Berry Hill Park: The creation of a "Destination Park" with new accessible footpaths, two new play areas, and a new building with a multi-functional space. Funded through the UK Government's Towns Fund, the project aims to boost the local economy by attracting more visitors and is expected to be completed in late 2025.[33]
  • Centenary Road (Poppy Fields): An Â£18 million project to deliver a third phase of 77 high-quality, affordable council homes, with work due for completion in May 2026.[33]
  • Warsop Health Hub: A brand-new community health hub that opened in the summer of 2024 and has already seen demand for memberships and swimming lessons surpass initial expectations.[33]

This narrative of investment and growth is the one the council's leadership consistently promotes. It is a story of ambition, of "improving people's lives," and of building a confident future for Mansfield.[33]

1.4 Reading the Ledger: Implications for the ICT Budget

The implications of this bifurcated financial reality for the ICT department are profound. The department must operate within two distinct financial ecosystems. The operational budget, which covers essential but unglamorous expenditures like hardware refreshes, software licensing, and routine maintenance, is subject to the intense pressures of the General Fund. The political will to allocate scarce resources to these areas is limited, particularly when viewed through the lens of public priorities.

The 2024 budget consultation provides a stark illustration of this political hierarchy. When residents were asked to rank the importance of council services, "Community Safety" and "Waste and Recycling" came first and second, respectively, with average scores of 2.76 and 3.77 (where 1 is most important). "Council Operations," the broad category that implicitly includes back-office functions like ICT, was ranked fifth out of eight with a score of 5.38, far below more visible, frontline services.[30] This data gives elected members a clear mandate to protect funding for police patrols and bin collections over internal IT systems.

This creates a situation where the core ICT infrastructure is likely underfunded, leading to the accumulation of technical debt. An ageing server, a network switch nearing its end-of-life, or an outdated software platform are the direct results of this financial logic. These are not seen as investments in public-facing priorities but as operational costs that can be deferred.

However, a second, parallel funding stream exists within the capital and regeneration projects. The construction of the Mansfield Connect hub, for example, will require a significant investment in digital infrastructure. The council's strategic ambition to become a "SMART town" cannot be realised without underlying technological investment. This is explicitly defined as using ICT to "increase operational efficiency, share information with the public and improve both the quality of services and the wellbeing for our residents".[34] This means that while a request to replace an old server from the operational budget might be rejected, a proposal to build a new, resilient data platform as an integral part of the Mansfield Connect project could be approved.

The critical skill for a technology leader in this environment is the ability to navigate this dual economy. Success depends on reframing necessary technological upgrades not as operational costs to be minimised, but as essential capital investments integral to the council's primary political narrative of regeneration and growth. The challenge is to shift proposals from the starved operational ledger to the well-funded capital one.

Section 2: Articulating ICT's Civic Value: From Technical Features to Public Good

In the politically charged environment of local government, the justification for any expenditure, particularly on back-office functions like ICT, cannot rest on technical merit alone. A 'faster login process' is a weak argument because it fails to connect with the council's core mission of public service. The key to securing resources and demonstrating strategic value lies in the ability to articulate technical solutions in non-technical, civic terms. This requires a deep understanding of the council's strategic vision and the ability to translate technical features into tangible public benefits, financial efficiencies, and political wins.

2.1 Deconstructing the Vision: "Towards 2030" and the "SMART Town" Aspiration

Mansfield District Council has clearly articulated its long-term ambitions in a series of strategic documents that provide the official language and priorities of the political leadership. The foundational document, "Towards 2030: A Strategy for Mansfield," sets out a vision for the district to be "ambitious, caring and confident... where everyone can prosper".[35] This vision is supported by five core ambitions: "Our communities," "Our organisation," "Our district," "Our environment," and "Our economy".[36] Within the "Our economy" theme, there is a specific, high-level priority to "Support the development of infrastructure, embracing technology and technological advances".[8]

The "Strategic Narrative" document, prepared for the district's investment plan, provides even greater clarity on the role of technology. It introduces the explicit aspiration for Mansfield to become a "SMART town". This is not a vague buzzword; the document defines it as using ICT to achieve three specific outcomes: "increase operational efficiency, share information with the public and improve both the quality of services and the wellbeing for our residents".[34] This definition provides a ready-made framework for justifying any technology project. Further reinforcement comes from the "Make it in Mansfield" strategy, a partnership initiative that includes the goal to "Improve connections across the Mansfield district through digital networks".[37]

These documents are not mere corporate paperwork; they are the political script. Using this languageâ€”"embracing technology," "SMART town," "improving wellbeing for our residents"â€”demonstrates an alignment with the council's approved strategic direction and transforms a technical request into a step towards a shared, politically endorsed goal.

2.2 A Lexicon for Civic Benefit: Learning from Best Practices

To effectively translate technical projects into this strategic language, it is instructive to examine how other public sector bodies have successfully made their case. These examples provide a powerful lexicon for articulating civic value.

Cornwall Council, for instance, justified a major internal digital transformation not by talking about server consolidation, but by highlighting three clear outcomes: "Â£2 million worth of savings," moving "1.5 million transactions online," and launching a county-wide "Digital Inclusion Strategy".[38] This framing connects the project directly to financial savings (a political priority), operational efficiency (a management priority), and social equity (a civic priority). Similarly, when hospitals in Southampton implemented a new digital system for managing patient discharges, the key metric for success was not the number of users, but the dramatic reduction in "lost bed days," which fell from 3,264 in March 2016 to 2,274 in March 2019.[39] This translated a software project into a tangible improvement in frontline public health deliveryâ€”freeing up hospital beds for those who need them most.[39]

Mansfield District Council has its own internal precedent for this approach. When the council procured the Jadu Continuum platform in 2019, the Strategic Director, Mariam Amos, did not focus on the technical features of the CMS. Instead, she justified the investment by highlighting the benefits to residents (a "smooth experience" that avoids the re-entering of information) and the financial benefit to the council (reducing "avoidable contact" at the contact centre).[1] This demonstrates that the council's leadership is already receptive to arguments framed around resident experience and operational efficiency.

2.3 Framing Proposals for Mansfield's Priorities: Safety, Housing, and Growth

The most effective arguments are those that align directly with the council's most pressing political and public priorities. The 2024 budget consultation provides a data-backed hierarchy of these priorities from the perspective of residents. Community Safety is the number one concern. Any ICT project that can be credibly framed as supporting this priority is immediately elevated in political importance. Conversely, projects framed solely as improving "Council Operations" (ranked fifth) are politically vulnerable.[30]

This requires a conscious effort to reverse-engineer proposals. The starting point should not be the technology, but the public priority.

Instead of: "We need to upgrade our data storage to accommodate growing file sizes."

Propose: "To support the Community Safety team's top priority of tackling anti-social behaviour, we must invest in a robust and secure data platform. This will allow us to effectively manage and store high-definition CCTV evidence, ensuring we can provide the police with the critical evidence they need to secure convictions and deliver on our promise to keep our neighbourhoods safe."

This same principle applies to the council's other major commitments, such as its extensive house-building programme.

Instead of: "We need to procure a new Customer Relationship Management system."

Propose: "To successfully deliver on our multi-million-pound commitment to build hundreds of new council homes, we need a modern housing management system. This platform will give our tenants 24/7 self-service access to report repairs and manage their tenancies online, which not only improves their experience but also frees up our housing officers' time to focus on providing direct support to our most vulnerable residents."

This approach reframes ICT from an internal cost centre into a strategic enabler of the council's most important public-facing goals. The following table provides a practical framework for this translation process, offering a model that can be applied to any technical initiative.

Section 3: Technology in the Theatre of Democracy

The Civic Centre is more than an office building; it is the physical theatre of local democracy. The technology that underpins the democratic process is therefore not merely administrative infrastructure. Its reliability, accessibility, and transparency are direct measures of the health of the council's relationship with its residents. A failure in these systems is not a technical glitch; it is a blow to public trust, transparency, and democratic accountability. An analysis of Mansfield District Council's current digital democratic infrastructure reveals a solid foundation but also a significant and surprising gap.

3.1 Public Access and Transparency: The Current State of Play

Mansfield District Council provides the fundamental building blocks of digital democratic access. The council's website serves as the primary portal for information, offering public access to the agendas and minutes of its various meetings, including Full Council, Cabinet, and Scrutiny committees.[43] The presence of separate links for documents pre- and post-April 2023 suggests a recent system change or upgrade, indicating that this area has received some level of technical attention.[43]

Beyond passive access to documents, the council has established a formal, albeit traditional, procedure for public participation. Residents can submit questions to be asked at meetings, a process that requires them to provide their question in writing to the Monitoring Officer no later than 12 noon, seven working days before the day of the meeting.[44]

The council's broader investment in its digital presence, particularly the 2019 adoption of the Jadu Continuum platform, signals a strategic understanding of the importance of digital channels for citizen interaction.[1] The stated goal of this project was to encourage residents to make full use of available digital services, establishing a foundational commitment to moving interactions online.[1]

3.2 A Digital Blind Spot? The Critical Ambiguity of Webcasting and E-Petitions

Despite this foundational investment in a modern digital platform, a comprehensive review of the council's online presence reveals a conspicuous absence of two key tools of modern digital democracy: live webcasting of council meetings and a system for e-petitions.

There is no evidence within the provided materials to suggest that Mansfield District Council (UK) live streams its public meetings. This absence is particularly striking when contrasted with the practices of other municipal bodies that share the "Mansfield" name. Research shows that the councils for Mansfield in Connecticut, Mansfield in Texas, and Mansfield Shire in Australia all actively promote the live streaming of their meetings, typically via platforms like YouTube.[45] This discrepancy, while a result of search term ambiguity, serves to highlight what has become a standard feature of democratic transparency elsewhere, and its apparent omission in the UK context is significant.

A similar gap exists regarding public petitions. While the council website details the specific, legally mandated process for citizens to trigger a referendum on the council's governance structure, which requires 4,046 signatures,[46] there is no general-purpose e-petitioning system for residents to raise and gather support for other local issues. Again, this stands in contrast to neighbouring authorities like Ashfield District Council, which offers a dedicated e-petitions portal on its website.[47] The lack of such a tool at Mansfield forces residents to rely on traditional, paper-based methods, creating a higher barrier to civic engagement.

3.3 Securing the Process: The Invisible Foundation of Public Trust

The absence of these high-visibility transparency tools creates a paradox. The council has invested significantly in a modern, public-facing digital platform capable of handling complex transactions and service requests.[1] It conducts digital consultations to inform its budget process.[30] This demonstrates a clear recognition of the power and efficiency of digital engagement. Why, then, would it omit relatively simple, low-cost, high-impact features like webcasting and e-petitions?

Several factors could explain this gap. It may be a question of cost, although the technology for webcasting has become increasingly affordable. It could be a technical capacity issue, with the ICT team focused on larger-scale projects. Or it could be a political choice, reflecting a desire to manage the flow of public scrutiny rather than open it up completely.

Regardless of the reason, this gap represents a political vulnerability for the council. In an era of increasing demand for transparency, the lack of live-streamed meetings could easily be framed by critics as evidence of a council that is not fully open to public oversight.

However, this vulnerability also presents a clear strategic opportunity for an incoming technology leader. Proposing the implementation of council meeting webcasting is a perfect example of a "quick win." It is a relatively low-cost project with a disproportionately high civic and political return. It directly addresses the council's stated strategic priority of maintaining "informed and transparent decision making".[42] It is a tangible, visible, and easily understood improvement to the democratic process. By identifying this gap and framing its solution in the language of democratic accountability, a candidate can demonstrate not only technical awareness but also a sophisticated understanding of the political landscape and an ability to deliver projects that generate significant political capital.

Section 4: Calibrating the Conversation: The Key Players and the Role

Understanding the formal and informal power structures within the council is critical to navigating the interview process and succeeding in the role. The title of the key contact, their position in the hierarchy, and the political oversight of the department dictate the nature of the conversation that must be had. It reveals whether the council is seeking a mechanic to maintain the existing engine or an engineer to help design the next one.

4.1 Identifying the Key Operational Leader: Steve Nowell, ICT and Infrastructure Manager

While the initial query mentioned a "Chris Jarvis," the most current and authoritative evidence points conclusively to Steve Nowell as the central figure in the council's technology operations. A senior management structure chart from August 2022 clearly identifies him with the title "ICT and Infrastructure Manager".[48] His role and longevity are further corroborated by his name appearing as the designated contact officer on numerous official decision notices and meeting minutes spanning from 2020 to 2023.[49] The references to "Chris Jarvis" are from much older documents, one from 2011,[50] and are likely outdated or related to a different individual or organisation. The focus of any technical conversation should therefore be calibrated to the role and responsibilities of Steve Nowell.

4.2 Deconstructing the Title: The Priorities of an Infrastructure-Focused Manager

The title "ICT and Infrastructure Manager" is highly revealing. It is not "Head of Digital Transformation," "Chief Technology Officer," or "Director of Innovation." The language is fundamentally operational, grounded, and focused on tangible assets. This title strongly implies that the core responsibilities of the role revolve around the foundational layers of the council's technology stack.

This is the domain of networks, servers, data centres, and physical hardware. It is about ensuring the reliability, uptime, and security of the core systems that the rest of the council depends on. Documents bearing Steve Nowell's name confirm this focus. In one report, he informed his superior that the "current in house ICT Helpdesk system no longer supported the aspirations of the ICT service" and recommended a modern, cloud-based replacement.[51] In another, he was the contact officer for a decision to procure a replacement Microsoft software agreement, noting that the software is "essential to support the ongoing digital aspirations of the Council".[52] His world is one of managing infrastructure, mitigating risk, and ensuring the continuity of service. In the analogy from the mandate, he is the chief mechanic, responsible for keeping the existing engine running smoothly and efficiently.

4.3 The Chain of Command: Mapping Political and Managerial Oversight

The ICT and Infrastructure Manager does not operate in a vacuum. The 2022 management chart shows that Steve Nowell reports to James Biddlestone , the "Head of People and Transformation".[48] This reporting line creates a crucial internal dynamic. The operational, infrastructure-focused manager reports to a senior leader whose very title is about strategic change and organisational development.

Above this managerial structure sits the political oversight. The council's cabinet structure places the Information Technology function within the "Corporate and Finance" portfolio.[53] This portfolio is held by Councillor Craig Whitby , who also serves as the Deputy Mayor .[54] This is a critical piece of intelligence. It means that any proposal for significant ICT expenditure must not only satisfy the operational requirements of the infrastructure manager and the strategic goals of the head of transformation, but it must also pass the rigorous financial scrutiny of the council's chief political steward of finance.

4.4 Tailoring the Pitch: Demonstrating Value to an Operational Leader

This organisational structureâ€”an operational manager reporting to a transformational director, all under the financial oversight of the Deputy Mayorâ€”provides the key to understanding the nature of the role being recruited. The council's high-level strategy documents are filled with the language of transformation, digital ambition, and the vision of a "SMART town".[34] Yet the person currently managing the department has a title firmly rooted in day-to-day operational reality.

This suggests a gap exists between the council's strategic aspirations and its current operational capacity and focus. The council is not simply hiring another pair of hands to manage servers; it is likely seeking to recruit a leader who can bridge this gap. The successful candidate must therefore demonstrate that they can operate effectively in both worlds.

To gain the confidence of an operational leader like Steve Nowell, a candidate must demonstrate robust technical competence and a clear understanding of the pressures of infrastructure management. They need to speak credibly about stability, security, resilience, and the challenges of managing legacy systems within a tight budget, referencing real-world examples like the need for a modern service desk system [51] or a reliable Microsoft software agreement.[52]

Simultaneously, to align with the priorities of James Biddlestone and the political leadership, the candidate must be fluent in the strategic language of civic value, operational efficiency, and digital transformation. They must be able to connect a proposal for a network upgrade to the delivery of better services for residents, and a server migration to the achievement of the council's financial and environmental goals as laid out in the "Towards 2030" strategy.[35]

The interview, therefore, is not a choice between being a mechanic or an engineer. It is a test of the ability to be both. The council is looking for a leader who can translate the high-level "transformation" agenda into a series of concrete, deliverable, and financially justifiable infrastructure projects. The role is to be the strategic-operational bridge, ensuring that the council's ambitious vision is built upon a solid and modern technological foundation.

Conclusion: Strategic Recommendations for the Interview

The preceding analysis indicates that Mansfield District Council is an organisation defined by a core tension between its financial constraints and its political ambitions. The ICT department sits at the epicentre of this tension, tasked with maintaining legacy systems under austerity while simultaneously being expected to enable a future-facing regeneration agenda. A candidate who can demonstrate a nuanced understanding of this dynamic and position themselves as a solution will be exceptionally well-placed. The following strategic recommendations are designed to translate this intelligence into a compelling interview performance.

  1. Synthesize the Core Contradiction: Begin by explicitly and confidently acknowledging the council's dual operating reality. Frame previous experience in terms of navigating such environmentsâ€”delivering efficiencies and cost-savings within a constrained operational budget while simultaneously identifying opportunities to leverage project-based capital funding for strategic technological upgrades. This demonstrates an immediate and sophisticated grasp of the council's primary challenge.
  2. Speak the Language of Civic Value: Rigorously avoid technical jargon unless specifically prompted. Proactively translate every technical concept into a civic benefit, using the council's own strategic vocabulary. Reference the goals of the "Towards 2030" strategy and the "SMART town" ambition.[34] Frame projects in terms of creating "thriving communities," enabling "transparent decision making," and delivering "value for money services". Use the framework provided in Table 1 to consistently link technical initiatives to political and public priorities.
  3. Identify and Propose a Quick Win: Use the identified ambiguity around council meeting webcasting as a strategic opportunity. Proactively highlight this as a potential low-cost, high-impact project that would "strengthen democratic transparency and build public trust." This accomplishes two goals: it demonstrates proactive, solution-oriented thinking, and it shows an understanding of how to deliver projects that provide tangible political value, aligning with the "transparent decision making" priority.[42]
  4. Position Yourself as the Bridge: The most critical recommendation is to frame the role as the essential link between the operational reality of the "ICT and Infrastructure Manager" [48] and the strategic goals of the "Head of People and Transformation".[48] Articulate a vision where robust, reliable, and secure infrastructure is the non-negotiable foundation upon which all digital transformation is built. Show that it is possible to manage the day-to-day nuts and bolts while keeping a clear focus on the larger strategic picture. The successful candidate will be the one who convinces the panel they are not just there to manage servers; they are there to help deliver the council's 2030 vision.
