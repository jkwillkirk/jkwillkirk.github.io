My Autistic

Personal Perspective on Autism

In my journey as an autistic individual, I've developed a unique cognitive landscape. While most people operate on the "neurotypical" system, tuned for social interactions and emotional understanding, my mind operates on a neurodiverse system. This cognitive system excels in logical and visual thinking, allowing for rapid and precise information processing. My passion for computers has granted me above-average knowledge in technology, driving my success in tech-related areas. My autism, particularly the Alexithymia byproduct, leads to a neutral and professional demeanour in my interactions, devoid of emotional biases. Consequently, I make decisions based on logical and objective criteria, ensuring an unbiased perspective. This emotional neutrality fosters fairness and objectivity in my assessments and judgments.

My Dyslexic

Personal Perspective on Dyslexia

My experience as someone with dyslexia feels like navigating life with a unique 3D processor in a predominantly 2D processor world. This distinctive cognitive perspective allows me to visualise complex systems, approach tech challenges from multiple angles, and manipulate abstract ideas within our minds. Thinking in three dimensions offers a valuable advantage, especially in fields like coding, engineering, design, or art, where the ability to envision intricate structures is paramount. I've learned to harness a unique cognitive approach where I mentally visualise problems, compare gathered information in a hologram-like fashion, and create lists of potential solutions; this ability, combined with our strong episodic memory, enables me to excel in learning through experiences and finding practical solutions to various challenges.
DR Maggie Aderin-Pocock on dyslexia.

Dyslexia and Teamwork for a Tech Job

In the tech industry, dyslexic individuals like me bring a distinctive cognitive advantage to any team. Our exceptional spatial thinking and the ability to visualise in three dimensions extend to understanding and mentally manipulating objects, making us valuable assets in tech fields like architecture, design, navigation, and even software development. Dyslexic team members excel in creating mental models, envisioning complex systems, and grasping the spatial relationships between various elements—skills particularly valuable in tech design and problem-solving.
